Overview of data

The dataset are student results from the Big Five Personality questionnaire. According to the Big Five Personality model, the 5 personalities are Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, Neuroticism, Openness and Extraversion (Goldberg, 1992).

The questionnaire is marked on a 5 point scale where 1=Disagree, 3=Neutral, 5=Agree (0=missed).

There are 382 records and 217 variables. All records are complete. 193 of the variables are text and 24 are integer. 1 record is an identifier.

Task Dimension reduction

Dimension reduction or feature selection is the process of selecting a subset of relevant features. The primary 3 reasons for using feature selection when constructing a model is that

  1. It improves the prediction performance of the predictors.
  2. Increases the performance of the model.
  3. Provides a better understanding of the underlying process that generated the data.

(Cunningham, 2008)

There are 3 primary methods to feature selection; filtering, using a wrapper method or embedded. Filtering is often univariate and considers the feature independently. The feature selection for our analysis will be a Principal Componenet Analysis (PCA) which is a wrapper method.

Hypothesis statement

H1: It is possible to measure the relationship between the selected factors and the manifest variables

H0: It is NOT possible to measure the relationship between selected factors and the manifest variables

Analysis plan

  • Select the 30 variables
  • Check correlations between variables using a correlation matrix plot.
  • Assess the Factorability of the data
    • Bartlett Test of Sphericity
    • Check Kaiser–Meyer–Olkin (KMO)
  • Determine the number of factors to extract
  • Do the dimension reduction
  • Factor rotation
  • Accept if Cronbach Alpha > 0.7 for each component

KMO is a better measure of factorability. The KMO tests to see if the partial correlations within your data are close enough to zero to suggest that there is at least one latent factor underlying in the variables. Results

A principal component analysis (PCA) was conducted on the 30 items with orthogonal rotation (varimax). Bartlett’s test of sphericity, Χ2(382) = 1.1652257^{-5} , p< .001, indicated that correlations between items were sufficiently large for PCA.

An initial analysis was run to obtain eigenvalues for each component in the data. Six components had eigenvalues over Kaiser’s criterion of 1 and in combination explained 56.1518015 of the variance. The scree plot was slightly ambiguous and showed inflections that would justify retaining either 6 or 3 factors. Given the large sample size, and the convergence of the scree plot and Kaiser’s criterion on three components, three components were retained in the final analysis. Component 1 represents a neuroticism, component 2 agreeableness, and component 3 extraversion.

All 3 personality traits from the Big Five Personality questionnaire had high reliabilities. Neuroticism cronbach’s α = 0.84. Agreeableness cronbach’s α = 0.82. Extraversion α = 0.81.

Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis.

Task Linear Regression

For the linear regression we will use the academic performance data.

Multiple linear regression are linear regression models that are used to build a linear relationship between one response or dependent variable and more than one predictor or independent variable.

The dependent variable is CC_S11, it is a numeric interval variable with values between 0 and 100, it is the result of an exam. The predictor variables are INTERNET, CR_S11 CC_PRO. INTERNET is a binary variable, ‘yes’ indicating that a student has access to internet and ’no’ that a student does not. CC_PRO and CR_S11 are exam result between the values of 0 and 100.

It is expected that students that have access to internet and have performed well in Citizen Competencies saber pro and critical reading saber 11 exam should perform better in the Citizen Competencies saber 11 exams than students who do not have access to internet and performed poorly in the Citizen Competencies saber pro exam.

Results

A multiple regression analysis was conducted to determine if a student’s score in CC_PRO and CR_S11O and whether they had access to internet could predict a student’s exam score CC_S11.

In order to include whether a student had access to the internet in the regression model it was recorded a dummy variable INTERNET (0 for no, 1 for yes)

Examination of the histogram, normal P-P plot of standardised residuals and the scatterplot of the dependent variable,exam score CC_S11, and standardised residuals showed that outliers existed. However, examination of the standardised residuals showed that none could be considered to have undue influence (95% within limits of -1.96 to plus 1.96 and none with Cook’s distance >1 as outlined in Field (2013).

Examination for multicollinearity showed that the tolerance and variance influence factor measures were within acceptable levels (tolerance >0.4, VIF <2.5 ) as outlined in Tarling (2008). The scatterplot of standardised residuals showed that the data met the assumptions of homogeneity of variance and linearity. The data also meets the assumption of non-zero variances of the predictors.

The Null hypothesis was rejected as the model as a whole (significant F statistic) was significant. However, a student’s access to the internet combined with other predictors was not shown to be significant in predicting a students result in CC_S11.

Task Logistic Regression

Logistic regression is to predict an outcome variable that is categorical (nominal) from one or more categorical (nominal) or continuous (interval or ratio) predictor variables.

The dependent variable is SCHOOL_NAT, it is a binary variable specifying whether the student went to a public or private school. The predictor variables are CR_S11 and CC_PRO and EDU_FATHER. CC_PRO and CR_S11 is an exam result between the values of 0 and 100. EDU_FATHER is a categorical variable specifying the highest level of formal education the father of the student achieved.

Students that have come from a private school are expected to have performed well in these exams due to their advantage. Students with a father that completed a higher level of education are thought to be more likely to have gone to a private school.

Hypothesis statement

H1: There will be a significant prediction on what type of school a student came from based on their CC_PRO and CR_S11 exam results and whether their father completed a high level of education.

H0: There will be no significant prediction on what type of school a student came from based on their CC_PRO and CR_S11 exam results andwhether their father completed a high level of education.

  • Create the model
  • Test the assumptions

Results

A binary logistic regression analysis was conducted with a student’s previous school type as the outcome variable (Private or Public) with the student’s fathers education as well as their results in CC_PRO and CR_S11 exams as predictors.

The data met the assumption for independent observations.

Examination for multicollinearity showed that the tolerance and variance influence factor measures were within acceptable levels (tolerance >0.4, VIF <2.5 ) as outlined in Tarling (2008).

Hosmer Lemeshow goodness of fit statistic did indicate issues with the assumption of linearity between the independent variables and the log odds of the model (χ2 (n=8)=20.445, p=0.009).

Due to the fail in Hosmer Lemeshow goodness of fit we accept the null hypothesis.

Conclusion

A better model to choose for predicting the exam result of CC_S11, is the model without INTERNET.

The reasons are:

  • The model is less complex (had one less variable)
  • The model was more significant as a whole.
  • The explained variance (adjusted R2) did not increase when INTERNET was added as a predictor.
  • The model had less outliers
